## The decisive comparison

Both b702 (mlc idle) and b703 (host idle) had jc_eff = 100.0%
throughout. They are apples-to-apples on the energy axis.

| Block | Pipeline | jc_eff | Energy mean | Energy sd | n_eng |
|------:|----------|-------:|------------:|----------:|------:|
| 702   | mlc      | 100.0% | 7014 mW     | 42        | 3554  |
| 703   | host     | 100.0% | 6982 mW     | 49        | 3551  |

**Gap (host - mlc): -32 mW.** Within the v7.5 section 6.3
plus-or-minus 50 mW noise-floor threshold.

Under jetson_clocks-effective measurement, host and MLC use
statistically indistinguishable energy at idle. The btest +155 mW
finding (which was the basis for v7.5 H4') was an artifact of
comparing measurements taken under free-running DVFS. The host
pipeline's slightly higher CPU utilization caused schedutil to
scale CPUs up more often than for the MLC pipeline; under
locked CPU frequency, this scaling difference disappears and the
energy axis collapses to noise.

**This falsifies v7.5 H4'** ("MLC saves host energy at idle under
vanilla scheduling"). The falsification is empirically clean,
not noise-driven (n>3500 per cell, sd ~45 mW, observed gap is
inside the noise floor).

The latency story is unchanged from v7.5:

- Bank-switch protocol overhead (3 I2C transactions per

- Magnitudes are tighter at long-duration jc-effective scale

The energy story is now SHARPER:

- Under proper measurement (jc-effective, long-duration),
  host and MLC are indistinguishable at idle.
- Razmi and Shojaei 2026 (arXiv:2602.21418) claim BOTH
  low-latency AND energy-efficient on-sensor inference. This

  energy claim is quantified at zero (statistically
  indistinguishable from host).
- The paper title can sharpen from "characterization of
  latency and energy" to "neither claim survives proper
  measurement."

## v7.7 amendment: §9 burst-protocol reformulation

Drafted ~13:00. The motivation: §9 was historically formulated
for the legacy continuous-streaming protocol, where ground truth
is window-by-window and the metric is aggregate accuracy. The
campaign uses servo_sweep with discrete 10-second burst cycles
(5 s motion + 5 s still), and the existing §9 formulation does
not cleanly capture per-phase performance.

The reformulation:

- Ground truth is now per-phase (each 5-second phase is one
  ground-truth label).
- The metric is per-phase classification: does the
  classifier emit the correct label predominantly during each
  phase?
- The "gap criterion" (which had been part of §9 since v6) is
  retired. The new per-phase metric obviates the gap criterion.

Phase mix is committed to 50% still / 50% motion (i.e., the
servo's idle-stimulus pattern). This is a deliberate choice:
real motion/still ratios in the field could vary widely, but
for the controlled experiment 50/50 is the cleanest signal-to-noise
test.

Operationally, §9 evaluation runs over the post-capture
trials.csv after the campaign completes. Per-phase ground truth
is established from sweep.log timestamps. The per-phase
classification fraction is computed and compared against a
per-phase threshold (90%).

Commit `f5bd702`, tag `v7-7`, DOI 10.5281/zenodo.20401671.
Back-fill `afbd3cd`.

## Post-capture analysis pipeline

The campaign produced raw .sal files but no CSV trial data;
the existing extract_latency_v7.py operates on a per-block
digital.csv. Three new scripts were written to bridge campaign
output to statistical analysis input:

### sal_to_csv.py

Uses logic2-automation (`from saleae import automation`) to drive
the Logic 2 GUI's Manager API. Loads each block's saleae.sal,
calls capture.export_raw_data_csv() for digital channels D0/D1/D2,
closes the capture, advances. Initial bug: relative paths failed
because Logic 2's CWD differs from the script's CWD. Fixed by
calling block_dir.resolve() to force absolute paths.

The script processes all 81 blocks in ~9 minutes (mostly Logic 2
load/export overhead). Produces ~120 MB of digital.csv files
which are gitignored as reproducible derivatives.

### batch_extract_trials.py

Per-block wrapper around extract_latency_v7.py. Reads each block's
block_metadata.json to determine the correct --pipeline and
--is-first-arm flags, runs extract_latency_v7.py with appropriate
arguments, captures the trials.csv output. Wall: 57 seconds for
81 blocks (dominated by Python startup per invocation; could be
parallelized but at this scale it doesn't matter).

## Statistical tests: all 5 testable hypotheses SUPPORTED

The pre-registered statistics module (`code/analysis/statistics.py`,
v7.4 Gate 6, unmodified since 2026-05-25) provides:

- `mann_whitney_u(x, y, alternative=...)` — one-sided MWU
- `hodges_lehmann(x, y)` and `hodges_lehmann_bootstrap_ci(x, y)` —
  effect-size estimator and 95% CI (10,000 resamples)
- `bootstrap_difference_of_medians(x_stress_host, x_stress_mlc, ...)` —
  the original §12 H2 function for difference-of-differences
- `tost_bootstrap(x, y, low_bound, high_bound)` — equivalence
  via bootstrap of median difference
- `holm_bonferroni(p_values, alpha)` — family-wise correction

A new runner script `code/analysis/run_confirmatory_stats.py`
loads the per-block trials.csv files, pools per-cell, calls the
pre-registered functions with appropriate arguments, applies
Holm-Bonferroni, and emits a JSON results file. Wall: ~7m45s,
dominated by Hodges-Lehmann bootstrap (each HL CI is n_boot ×
O(n*m) where n*m ≈ 280,000 for our sample sizes).

### One algebraic identity worth documenting

H3' wants the bootstrap CI of (Δ_MLC − Δ_host) where Δ =
contention − idle for each pipeline. The pre-registered §12 H2
function `bootstrap_difference_of_medians` computes
(Δ_stress − Δ_nostress) where Δ = median(host) − median(mlc) at
each stress level. These are different quantities.

Algebraic identity: with arguments arranged as
(x_stress_host=mlc_contention, x_stress_mlc=host_contention,
x_nostress_host=mlc_idle, x_nostress_mlc=host_idle), the function
produces
[median(mlc_contention) − median(host_contention)] −
[median(mlc_idle) − median(host_idle)]

which equals (Δ_MLC − Δ_host) by reassociation. The argument
arrangement is the only nontrivial step; the pre-registered
function does the bootstrap correctly once given the right
inputs. This identity is documented in the runner source so an
auditor can verify the swap.

### The H5' + H6' control pair

The two control hypotheses together establish the experimental
design's discriminative power:

- H5' supports: CPU stress is NULL on the latency axis.
- H6' supports: CPU stress is STRONGLY POSITIVE on the energy axis.

Combined inference: the host pipeline's latency under I²C
contention (H2') is NOT explained by generic CPU stress. The
contention condition adds latency in a way that CPU stress does
not, ruling out the most obvious confound.

This was the most carefully-designed part of the pre-reg, and
the data validates the design. The discriminative power claim is
defensible to a reviewer: H5'+H6' demonstrate the experimental
apparatus can detect stress on one axis and reject it on another.

## Multimodal latency distributions: an honest observation

A finding worth honest documentation, not premature explanation.

Both mlc and mlc-binary pipelines show multimodal latency
distributions across conditions:

- mlc idle: bimodal at ~480 µs and ~675 µs (~180 µs separation)
- mlc-binary idle: tri-modal at ~50 µs, ~230 µs, ~450 µs
- mlc-binary i2c-contention: dominant mode at ~50 µs, secondary
  cluster around 230 µs (different mix than idle)

Three post-hoc explanations were investigated and disproven:

1. **Parity (every-other-trial alternation)** — disproven: the
   cluster assignment does not correlate with trial index parity.
2. **Stimulus direction (motion_to_still vs still_to_motion)** —
   disproven: both directions produce both clusters within a
   single block.
3. **D0 inter-edge gap (the gap between consecutive MLC
   classifications)** — disproven: same gap value (e.g.,
   3530 ms, an integer multiple of 706.5 ms) produces both
   FAST and SLOW clusters in the same block.

The origin of the multimodality is unknown. Investigation paused
because the next obvious step would be kernel-level
instrumentation (perf, ftrace, IRQ timing) which is out of scope
for tonight and possibly out of scope for the paper. The honest
reporting strategy: document the observation, note that we
cannot currently explain it, propose kernel-level instrumentation
as future work.

This is the kind of finding that the pre-registration framework
rewards. The pre-registered hypotheses (H1'-H6') are unaffected
by the multimodality — they're statements about medians and
distributions, not about modes. The multimodality is an
exploratory observation, clearly labeled as such, and not load-
bearing for any pre-registered claim.

### MLC 706.5 ms intrinsic decision cadence (independent finding)

Separate from the multimodality: the inter-edge gaps in D0
(rising edges of INT1 from the MLC) are quantized to integer
multiples of 706.5 ms. For one example block (b005 mlc-binary
idle), the gap distribution shows: 3530.1 ms (706.5×5), 4236.2 ms
(706.5×6), 6354.5 ms (706.5×9), 7060.6 ms (706.5×10).

706.5 ms = ~1/4 of the configured 75-sample × 26 Hz window
period (75/26 ≈ 2.885 s, divided by 4 ≈ 0.721 s — close to 706.5).
The exact divisor depends on the MLC's internal scheduling that
is not fully documented in AN5259. The empirical observation is
clean: the MLC fires its decision interrupt only on multiples of
706.5 ms from some internal start time.

This finding is worth a sentence in the paper's results section
and is unrelated to the multimodality of latency. It explains
why the campaign's stimulus protocol (10 s burst cycles) produces
a discrete set of D0 inter-edge gaps rather than a continuous
distribution: the MLC's quantized firing cadence interacts with
the stimulus timing.

### mlc-binary is genuinely 0-I²C-transaction

A small but important methodology correction. Throughout earlier
sessions, mlc-binary was assumed to be "an MLC pipeline with a
binary classifier configuration" — i.e., still doing the
3-transaction bank-switch read but with a 2-class model.

Reading the actual C source today (`code/jetson/mlc_pipeline/latency_test_mlc_binary.c`)
showed otherwise: the binary toggles D1 unconditionally on every
INT1 rising edge, WITHOUT any I²C read. The "binary" in the name
refers to "binary GPIO toggle" not "binary classifier."

Implications:
- mlc-binary's measured latency is pure kernel-side
  interrupt-to-GPIO write time, which is itself multimodal
  (~50 µs and ~230 µs modes from kernel scheduling, not from
  MLC silicon).
- mlc-binary is therefore the right comparison for "what's the
  minimum possible latency if we don't read the MLC at all" —
  it isolates the MLC silicon's classification latency from the
  bank-switch read protocol.
- The comparison mlc vs mlc-binary quantifies the bank-switch
  read overhead specifically. Median mlc idle 681.5 µs minus
  median mlc-binary idle 231.9 µs = ~450 µs attributable to the
  3-transaction read protocol.

This correction was made and committed before any paper text was
written that depended on the earlier (incorrect) assumption.

## Methodological lessons recorded (additions to morning session's)

1. **Retrospective pre-registration with full disclosure is
   procedurally defensible.** v7.9 was minted AFTER the analysis-
   side gap was closed (extract_latency_v7.py extended to mlc-binary)
   but BEFORE any statistical test was run on the affected data.
   The honest disclosure language in v7.9 makes the timing
   explicit. An auditor can verify that the timestamp ordering
   (extract change → v7.9 DOI → statistical tests) preserves the
   pre-registration discipline's central claim: "the analysis
   logic was fixed before the analysis was run."

2. **Pre-registered scripts must be kept synchronized with
   pre-registered designs.** v7.5 added mlc-binary at the
   campaign level without updating the extractor. The mismatch
   surfaced only at the analysis stage. A pre-flight checklist
   item for future amendments: "does the analysis code path
   exist for every pre-registered condition?"

3. **Pre-registered predictions falsify cleanly.** v7.5 Change 1
   predicted a smaller energy delta under mode 3; H6' falsified
   that prediction with high confidence (CI is far from the
   predicted-smaller direction). The framework worked as
   designed: a specific empirical prediction was made, it was
   tested under controlled conditions, the data refuted it. No
   re-interpretation or special pleading is needed.

4. **Honest exploratory observations strengthen rather than
   weaken the paper.** The multimodality finding, presented as
   "we observed clusters whose origin we cannot currently
   explain," is more credible than a forced explanation would
   have been. The pre-registration framework gives us cover to
   report it honestly: the pre-registered claims are unaffected,
   the exploratory observation is clearly labeled.

5. **Algebraic identities can preserve pre-registration
   discipline.** H3' was tested via the §12 H2 function with
   arguments arranged to produce the right quantity. The
   alternative (writing a new bootstrap function) would have
   been cleaner code but procedurally messier (new code path
   not pre-registered). The chosen path uses pre-registered
   code with a documented identity — auditable and preserves
   the discipline.

6. **The discriminative-control design (H5'+H6') is paper-grade.**
   Designing the pre-reg with two control hypotheses — one null
   on the axis of interest, one positive on a different axis —
   pays off in publication-ready discriminative power. The
   reviewer's natural skepticism ("could this be a generic stress
   confound?") is preemptively addressed by H5'+H6' as a pair.
