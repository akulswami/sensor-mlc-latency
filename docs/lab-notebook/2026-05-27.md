## H7' viability — the question

H7' was pre-registered in v7.6 Change 6 (2026-05-26, Zenodo DOI
10.5281/zenodo.20400025) as:

> H7' (classifier stability degradation under bus contention): The
> per-cell classifier-stability rate (fraction of trials with exactly
> 1 D1 per window) is lower under i2c-contention than under idle, for
> the MLC bank-switch pipeline.
>
> H7'₀: stability(MLC | i2c-contention) ≥ stability(MLC | idle)
> H7'₁: stability(MLC | i2c-contention) <  stability(MLC | idle)
>
> Test: one-sided proportion test (chi-square or Fisher's exact at
> the per-trial level), Holm-Bonferroni corrected across the full set
> of confirmatory hypotheses.

The viability question: does the committed campaign data expose
per-trial stability outcomes, or would we need to re-process saleae.sal
files (or even re-collect data) to test this?

Two scenarios:
- (a) `trials.csv` already records per-trial stability somehow → test
  is trivial
- (b) `trials.csv` only records included trials (with their latency) →
  need to re-process digital.csv to count D1 transitions per window
- (c) Even digital.csv is insufficient → need to re-collect with
  explicit per-window logging

Investigation showed (a) is the actual case. The `extract_latency_v7.py`
extractor already classifies every per-trial outcome via the
`exclusion_reason` field. Per the source (lines 250-340), the flow is:

1. Count D1 rising edges in (t_stim, t_next_stim]:
   - 0 → `no_d1_in_window` (UNSTABLE: zero D1 in window)
   - >=2 → `multiple_d1_in_window` (UNSTABLE: oscillation)
   - exactly 1 → continue (STABLE)
2. THEN check D0 pairing (`multiple_d0_before_d1`, `no_d0_between_stim_and_d1`)
3. THEN check latency bound (`latency_exceeds_100ms`)

Reasons 2 and 3 are checked AFTER the trial already has exactly 1 D1.
So a trial classified `multiple_d0_before_d1` IS stable in the H7'
sense — it had exactly 1 D1, just preceded by >1 D0. This matters for
the operationalization: H7' "stable" = exclusion_reason NOT in
{`no_d1_in_window`, `multiple_d1_in_window`}, NOT "included = True".

This distinction was made explicit in the v7.10 amendment text and in
the runner script docstring.

## H7' result interpretation

The pre-registered direction is falsified. The two-sided test is
marginal (p=0.076, just above the conventional 0.05 threshold), but
the observed direction is opposite to the prediction.

| Condition | Stable | Unstable | Stability |
|---|---|---|---|
| mlc / idle | 525 | 15 (2 no_d1 + 13 multi_d1) | 97.22% |
| mlc / i2c-contention | 534 | 6 (1 + 5) | 98.89% |
| mlc / stress (reference, not in H7' test) | 527 | 13 (1 + 12) | 97.59% |

A few honest observations from this table:

**Observation 1: mlc/idle is the outlier, not mlc/i2c-contention.** Both
stressed conditions (i2c-contention at 98.89%, cpu-stress at 97.59%)
show slightly HIGHER stability than idle. If H7' had been worded
generically as "stability under stress is lower than at idle," it
would still be falsified. The contention case is the strongest of the
three "stress is fine" comparisons.

**Observation 2: the absolute unstable counts are small.** 15 vs 6 in
the H7' test cells. The two-sided p of 0.076 is close to significance
but the small counts make any direction-claim statistically modest.
The clean part of the finding is the falsification of the predicted
direction; the magnitude of the contrary effect is open to noise
concerns.

**Observation 3: multi_d1_in_window is the dominant unstable mode.**
Across all three conditions, multiple_d1 outnumbers no_d1 by roughly
6:1 (30 vs 4 across the three cells). This is consistent with the
v7.6 §11 restatement diagnosis: at long-duration scale, the dominant
exclusion is the MLC's classifier flickering between motion/still as
the silicon's 706 ms cadence interacts with stimulus windows.
"Instability" in this experiment is overwhelmingly "the silicon
fires twice during a stimulus window" rather than "the silicon
misses a stimulus entirely."

**Candidate mechanism (not pre-registered, recorded for paper
exploratory discussion):** steady-state load may reduce timing-edge
artifacts. Under idle, the system has more headroom for occasional
CPU spikes (background daemons, kernel housekeeping) that may cause
the MLC pipeline to read MLC0_SRC at unexpected times relative to
the silicon's decision window. Under contention or sustained CPU
load, the system is in a more consistent state, which may smooth
out these timing-edge events. This is a hypothesis for future work,
not a paper claim.

## Holm-Bonferroni across the full family

The pre-reg specifies multiplicity correction "across the full set of
confirmatory hypotheses". The family is {H1', H2', H3', H6', H7'}.
(H5' is TOST equivalence, conventionally excluded from p-value-based
correction; reported separately.)

Result table (sorted by raw p, ascending):

| Rank | Hypothesis | Raw p | Holm-adjusted p | Threshold | Rejected? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | H6' | 0 (<float64 epsilon) | 0 | 0.0100 | YES |
| 2 | H1' | 1.87 × 10⁻¹⁷⁰ | 7.46 × 10⁻¹⁷⁰ | 0.0125 | YES |
| 3 | H2' | 2.33 × 10⁻¹⁷⁰ | 6.99 × 10⁻¹⁷⁰ | 0.0167 | YES |
| 4 | H3' | 1.00 × 10⁻⁴ | 2.00 × 10⁻⁴ | 0.0250 | YES |
| 5 | H7' | 0.9874 | 0.9874 | 0.0500 | NO |

The other four hypotheses remain rejected at family-wise α=0.05 after
the H7' addition to the family. H7' alone fails to reject. This is
the cleanest possible outcome for the multiplicity story: the
positive findings are robust to the addition of a new control test;
the new control test itself does not contaminate the others.

## Methodological lessons recorded (additions to prior days')

1. **Per-trial stability is exposed by the existing exclusion-reason
   field; no re-extraction needed.** This is a small but important
   point about analysis-pipeline design: the §11 exclusion
   categorization done at extraction time happens to capture exactly
   the per-trial outcome required for H7'. The lucky alignment was
   discovered by reading the extractor source carefully rather than
   assumed from data shape. Future per-trial stability tests on
   similar pipelines should check the existing categorization first
   before designing new processing.

2. **Offline-to-live cross-check is a useful pre-flight discipline.**
   Building the runner script offline (on a zip snapshot) and
   predicting exact numbers (97.22%, 98.89%, p=0.9874) before
   running on live data gave a clean reproducibility test. If the
   live numbers had differed, the discrepancy would have surfaced
   immediately and forced debugging before committing. The cost was
   minimal (a few minutes of zip extraction and offline scripting);
   the assurance is real.

3. **Procedurally clean falsifications strengthen the paper, not
   weaken it.** v7.10's falsification follows the same template as
   v7.5 (H1) and v7.6 (H4'). Each one's procedural cleanliness
   reinforces the methodology-contribution framing of the paper:
   pre-registration with Zenodo timestamps is not just a defensive
   posture, it is an active scientific discipline that produces
   honest results.

4. **The two-sided p=0.076 is the most honest part of the H7'
   reporting.** A weaker discipline might have either (a) ignored
   the marginal two-sided result and claimed "no effect" or (b)
   reported the two-sided p=0.076 as "trending toward significance
   in the wrong direction" with implicit suggestion of effect. The
   pre-registered one-sided test in the predicted direction is the
   formal answer (p=0.987 → cannot reject), and the two-sided p is
   reported alongside as a reference. Neither test produced a
   significant result in the predicted direction; the data favors
   the opposite direction but not significantly at α=0.05.

5. **The amendment template scales down well.** v7.10 is ~150 lines
   vs v7.6's ~250 because the falsification doesn't trigger
   methodology changes. The template's structure (Status, Reason,
   Empirical finding, Change, What is NOT changed, Mechanism
   observation, Falsification record, Substantive implication,
   External timestamp) accommodates both small and large amendments
   without redundancy.
