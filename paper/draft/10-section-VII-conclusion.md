# §VII. Conclusion

We measured wire-level stimulus-to-decision latency for three pipelines on an NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano + STMicroelectronics LSM6DSOX edge platform under idle, I²C bus contention, and CPU stress conditions. Against the commercial intuition that on-sensor inference is faster, the host-side decision-tree pipeline achieves a 2.1× lower median latency than the standard MLC bank-switch read pipeline at idle (321.7 µs vs 681.5 µs) and a 2.3× lower median latency under I²C bus contention (574.5 µs vs 1,325.4 µs). The mlc-binary variant — which omits the I²C read entirely and toggles the decision GPIO on every MLC interrupt — isolates the cost of the bank-switch read protocol at approximately 1,276 µs under contention. The three-transaction I²C read sequence, not the silicon's classification, is the dominant latency contributor.

The control results refine the safety-critical reading. CPU stress is detectable on energy (+3,420 mW) but null on host latency and null on classifier reliability; I²C contention is significant on latency but null on classifier reliability. The safety-critical specification axis is which resource is contended — not whether the platform is "under stress" in some aggregate sense. We additionally document a previously unreported 706.5 ms intrinsic MLC decision cadence that contributes a 0–706.5 ms quantization wait to system-level stimulus-to-decision latency, dominating the wire-level differences this paper characterizes.

Three of the originally pre-registered hypotheses were falsified during the study, including the headline directional claim. Each falsification is preserved on the public record via Zenodo-timestamped amendments. The pre-registration framework's value is demonstrably not that the original hypotheses survived but that the falsifications are documentably honest. Code, data, and the full pre-registration chain are available at github.com/akulswami/sensor-mlc-latency.
