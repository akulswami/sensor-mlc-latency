# §VI. Discussion

## VI.A The I²C read protocol is the dominant latency contributor

The headline finding — host-side classification is 2.1–2.3× faster than the on-sensor MLC under every tested condition — is mechanistically explained by Fig. 1's panel (c): the mlc-binary pipeline, which performs zero I²C transactions on the decision path (toggling D1 unconditionally on every INT1 rising edge), reaches a median latency of 49.4 µs under contention and 70.2 µs under CPU stress. The difference between mlc and mlc-binary under identical conditions isolates the I²C read overhead: roughly 1,276 µs under contention (1,325.4 − 49.4) and 476 µs under stress (546.1 − 70.2). These costs are not the silicon's classification time; they are the bank-switch read protocol's three-transaction sequence (write `FUNC_CFG_ACCESS`, read `MLC0_SRC`, write `FUNC_CFG_ACCESS` back) competing for bus arbitration.

Two structural consequences follow. First, any platform that uses the LSM6DSOX MLC over I²C inherits this overhead by construction: the bank-switch protocol is not optional and not bypassable for the standard read path. Second, the overhead scales with bus contention rather than with classifier complexity: a more sophisticated MLC configuration (more classes, deeper trees) would not change the per-decision I²C cost, but a busier I²C bus does.

The mlc-binary variant is the floor for what on-sensor inference could deliver if the read protocol were bypassed — for example, by a 1-bit "decision changed" GPIO that the silicon could toggle directly, or by SPI access at higher clock rates. We are not proposing a hardware change; we are noting that the silicon's classification appears to be fast, and the host-MLC latency gap is overwhelmingly a bus-protocol artifact, not a classifier-architecture artifact.

## VI.B Implications for safety-critical edge ML

A naive reading of "on-sensor inference is faster than host inference" would suggest that for low-latency safety-critical loops — such as the exoskeleton control application motivated in §I [REF-RAZMI-2026] — the MLC should be preferred over a host classifier. Our results invert that conclusion on the wire-level latency axis, at least for the LSM6DSOX over I²C on a representative edge platform. The host pipeline reaches its decision 359 µs earlier at idle and 753 µs earlier under bus contention, and is equivalence-tested null against CPU stress (H5' SUPPORTED). The MLC pipeline incurs an extra 391 µs of contention-induced latency relative to the host (H3' SUPPORTED).

The control results refine this further. H6' confirms that CPU stress is detectable on the energy axis (+3,420 mW), even when it is null on the host's latency axis (H5'). H7' establishes that I²C contention degrades latency (H2', H3') without degrading classifier reliability (H7' falsified — the stable-trial rate is, if anything, slightly higher under contention). The substantive claim emerges from the joint reading: **"stress" is not a single thing.** A system specification that treats CPU stress and bus contention as a unified failure mode will mis-budget for both. The relevant axes are:

- **CPU stress** is significant on **energy** (H6') but null on **latency** (H5', host) and null on **classifier reliability** (H7' for the contention case; CPU stress was tested as a separate condition).
- **I²C bus contention** is significant on **latency** (H2', H3') but null on **classifier reliability** (H7').

For safety-critical specification: if the deployed environment has bus contention (other I²C devices on the same bus contending for arbitration), the latency budget must account for it. If the deployed environment has CPU saturation but a clean bus, the host pipeline's latency is essentially unaffected. Bundling these into a single "stress margin" risks over-provisioning for one axis while under-provisioning for the other.

## VI.C Multimodal latency distributions

§V.B documented multimodal structure in both MLC pipelines at idle, with secondary modes separated by hundreds of microseconds. The mlc/idle distribution's mean (866.6 µs) substantially exceeds its median (681.5 µs); the mlc-binary/idle distribution shows three visible modes at approximately 60, 240, and 470 µs. Under bus contention or CPU stress, these distributions collapse to tighter unimodal forms.

We do not present a confirmed mechanism. Candidate explanations include: (a) idle-state I²C bus arbitration on the Jetson's `i2c-tegra194` driver having multiple equilibrium timings between conflicting wake-up paths, (b) the gpiod write path through `/dev/gpiochip0` traversing different kernel call sequences depending on whether the underlying chardev poll mechanism is in steady-state or recently-armed, or (c) some interaction between the MLC's 706.5 ms internal cadence (§V.C) and the kernel's microsecond-resolution interrupt-arrival timing. Distinguishing these requires ftrace instrumentation of the I²C driver and the gpiochip event flow at the kernel level, which lies outside the scope of this wire-level study.

The methodological contribution is the observation itself: per-trial wire-level latency measurements reveal substructure that median/mean reporting cannot. For safety-critical specification of "what is the worst latency we will observe with probability 1 − ε," the bimodal upper mode is the relevant quantity, not the median. The p95 latency for mlc/idle (1,781 µs) is 2.6× the median (681.5 µs), a factor that disappears in a unimodal-Gaussian assumption.

## VI.D The MLC's intrinsic decision cadence

§V.C reported the 706.5 ms cadence quantization in D0 inter-edge timing — equal to one-quarter of the 75-sample × 26 Hz window. We have not seen this cadence documented in ST's application notes [REF-AN5259] or in the LSM6DSOX datasheet. Its practical consequence: even if a stimulus arrives at time t, the MLC's first opportunity to fire INT1 in response is the next 706.5 ms boundary. The expected wall-clock waiting time from stimulus arrival to MLC response is therefore uniformly distributed between 0 and 706.5 ms in the absence of synchronization, with a mean of 353 ms.

This is invisible on the wire-level latency axis we measured (which times from D0 to D1, not from stimulus to D0) but it is the dominant contributor to **stimulus-to-decision** latency at the system level. For applications where the stimulus is a real-world event with no synchronization to the MLC's internal clock, the 0–706.5 ms quantization-wait is a structural floor below which on-sensor inference cannot operate. The 1–2 ms wire-level latency differences this paper characterizes are a second-order effect against that backdrop.

## VI.E Limitations

This study is constrained in five ways. First, **single platform**: all measurements were collected on a single NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano with a single LSM6DSOX breakout. The numerical values are specific to this combination; the structural findings (I²C-read protocol dominates, multimodal distributions, 706.5 ms cadence) should transfer to similar ARM-edge + ST-MEMS combinations but require empirical confirmation. Second, **single sensor IC family**: results characterize STMicroelectronics MLCs as deployed in the LSM6DSOX. Other vendors' on-sensor inference (Bosch BHI260, Analog Devices ADXL367 ASIC variants) are out of scope. Third, **single bus protocol**: I²C at 400 kHz, not SPI. SPI access would substantially reduce per-transaction overhead and may invert the host-vs-MLC ordering for some configurations. Fourth, **fixed accelerometer ODR**: 208 Hz throughout, set by the MLC config's training-window assumption. Higher ODRs are feasible but would change the polling-loop tradeoff for the host pipeline. Fifth, **single MLC configuration**: a 2-class motion/still classifier with 75-sample windows. More sophisticated MLC configurations (8 classes, deeper trees, multiple decision trees) would not change the per-decision I²C cost but would change the silicon's internal classification latency, which this study does not separately measure.

The pre-registration's planned chrt+taskset scheduling ablation (v7.5 Change 7; SCHED_FIFO priority 99 with `taskset -c 5`) was not activated for the confirmatory campaign; the orchestrator modification required to invoke pipelines under RT scheduling remained an unstaged transformation, and no amendment after v7.5 introduced the `--scheduling` flag. Btest-scale data suggested RT scheduling could reduce MLC bank-switch i2c-contention latency from ~1,534 µs to ~796 µs; confirming or refuting this at confirmatory scale, and characterizing whether the gpiod userspace jitter visible in mlc-binary's bimodal idle distribution can be eliminated, is the most concrete next step.
